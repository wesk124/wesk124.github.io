Bridging Systems Engineering and Machine Learning

Sheng Wei · March 26, 2026 · 6 min read

After nearly seven years of building satellite networking infrastructure at Hughes Network Systems, I made a deliberate pivot toward machine learning. The transition wasn't as jarring as some might expect — systems engineering and ML share more DNA than they appear to on the surface.

The Unexpected Overlap

At its core, systems engineering is about managing complexity under constraints: latency budgets, memory limits, real-time deadlines. Machine learning has its own flavor of the same challenge: compute budgets, model size, inference latency. When I first started working through PyTorch tutorials, I kept finding myself thinking in terms I already knew.

Multithreaded pipeline design maps directly onto ML data loaders. FPGA DMA buffer management isn't so different from understanding GPU memory hierarchies. Packet queuing algorithms rhyme with batch scheduling strategies. The vocabulary is different, but the intuition transfers.

What Systems Engineers Bring to ML

Most ML practitioners come from a math or data-science background. That gives them strong theoretical grounding, but production ML systems require something else: the ability to reason about what's happening at the hardware level.

  • Profiling instincts. Knowing when to reach for cProfile, torch.profiler, or just a well-placed timer is a skill built over years of debugging real-time systems.
  • Failure mode thinking. In satellite comms, every component fails. You design for graceful degradation from the start. This mindset is undervalued in ML systems where flaky inference pipelines are treated as acceptable.
  • Low-level debugging. Being comfortable with GDB, memory leak analysis, and DPDK packet traces translates to confidence when triaging CUDA OOM errors or NCCL hangs.

What I Had to Unlearn

The hardest adjustment was embracing uncertainty. In embedded systems, code is deterministic. A packet either arrives or it doesn't. Algorithms either terminate or they don't. Machine learning demands a different epistemology: you iterate, you measure, you accept that "good enough" is defined statistically.

Early on, I kept wanting to understand every weight update. That instinct — trace everything to its source — is valuable for debugging but paralyzing for research. Learning to trust the loss curve and move on was genuinely difficult.

The Path Forward

My current focus at UW's MSEE program is the intersection I find most interesting: efficient inference systems. How do you deploy a large model on edge hardware? How do you quantize without destroying accuracy? These problems live squarely at the boundary of systems and ML — and I think engineers with backgrounds in both will have an outsized impact on how AI gets deployed at scale.

If you're a systems engineer thinking about making a similar transition, my advice is simple: start building. The theory will follow the practice. Your background isn't a liability — it's a lens that most people in the field don't have.
